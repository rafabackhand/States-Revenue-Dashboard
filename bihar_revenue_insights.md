# Bihar Revenue Analysis — Insights

*Fiscal year 2023-24 unless otherwise noted. All values in ₹ Crore. Peer group: Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, Odisha, Jharkhand, West Bengal.*

---

## 1. Executive summary

- Bihar's total revenue receipts in FY 2023-24 were **₹1,93,347 Cr (rank 9 of 28 states)**, but on a per-capita basis Bihar is **dead last at ₹18,573/person** against a major-state median of ₹17,136 and a peer average of ₹29,429.
- **72% of Bihar's revenue comes from the Centre** (Share in Union Taxes ₹1,13,604 Cr + Grants ₹26,125 Cr), versus a peer average of 51%. Own-source revenue finances only 28% of Bihar's budget.
- Bihar's **State Own Tax Revenue (SOTR) per capita is ₹4,646** — rank 27 of 28. If Bihar collected SOTR at the peer per-capita rate, SOTR would rise by **₹68,226 Cr (+141%)**.
- The biggest *tractable* gaps are **Non-Tax Revenue (+₹35,057 Cr)**, **SGST (+₹21,643 Cr)**, and **Stamps & Registration (+₹3,671 Cr)** — together worth roughly **₹60,000 Cr/year** in recurrent revenue.
- Over the last decade, Bihar's SOTR grew at **9.25% CAGR vs 10.29% nationally**. Only Non-Tax Revenue (+13.03% vs national 9.17%) and Motor Vehicle Tax (+14.88% vs 11.05%) have outperformed the nation.

### Dependence on the Centre
- **Central transfers = 72.3% of Bihar's total revenue** (Share in Union Taxes 58.8% + Grants 13.5%)
- **Own revenue = 27.7%** (SOTR 25.0% + Non-Tax 2.7%)
- **Peer average own-revenue share = 49.1%** — nearly double Bihar's
- Share in Union Taxes is the one metric where Bihar punches above weight: **rank 2 absolute** and **rank 16 per-capita**, marginally *above* the peer per-capita average. The Finance Commission devolution formula (which weights population, per-capita income gap, and backwardness) is doing its intended job.

## 3. Top three opportunity areas

### Opportunity 1 — Non-Tax Revenue: **+₹35,057 Cr gap (largest)**
- **Bihar today:** ₹5,257 Cr, ₹505 per capita (rank 27 of 28)
- **Peer average:** ₹20,422 Cr, ₹3,873 per capita (7.7× Bihar)
- **Potential at peer per-capita rate:** ₹40,314 Cr
- **10-year CAGR:** Bihar 13.0% vs national 9.2% — Bihar is the rare outperformer here, off a very low base
- **What Non-Tax Revenue includes:** mining/mineral royalties, interest receipts, dividends from state PSUs, user charges (water, electricity duty, road tolls), fees on administrative services, forestry receipts, irrigation charges
- **Policy levers:**
  - **Mining royalties** — Bihar has minor minerals (sand, stones, bricks) that peer states (Odisha, Jharkhand, MP) monetise aggressively. Digitise e-permits, enforce against illegal mining, reauction expired leases.
  - **User charges on public services** — water supply, irrigation, electricity duty are heavily subsidised in Bihar. Gradual tariff revision with lifeline-tier protection for below-poverty-line users.
  - **Dividends from state PSUs** — enforce dividend policies on profitable PSUs; unwind chronic loss-makers.
  - **Interest receipts** — active cash management (consolidate idle balances across departments into a treasury single account) can add hundreds of Cr/year at current rates.
  - **Data caveat:** this line needs a sector split from the workbook sheets we haven't yet extracted to target reforms precisely. See §5.

### Opportunity 2 — SGST: **+₹21,643 Cr gap**
- **Bihar today:** ₹27,678 Cr (59% of SOTR — an unusually high concentration)
- **Peer average:** ₹38,016 Cr (46% of SOTR)
- **Potential at peer per-capita rate:** ₹49,321 Cr
- **6-year CAGR (2017-18 → 2023-24):** Bihar 26.5% — above national average, i.e., the gap is narrowing but the base was low
- **Structural note:** SGST ultimately depends on consumption within the state. Bihar's per-capita GSDP is roughly half the peer average, so a portion of this gap is genuinely a function of economic size, not tax administration. A realistic capture is probably 40-60% of the headline gap — call it **₹8,000–13,000 Cr**.
- **Policy levers:**
  - **Taxpayer base expansion** — unregistered informal traders below the ₹40L/₹20L threshold; outreach + simplified composition scheme uptake.
  - **Return-filing compliance** — Bihar's GSTR-3B filing lag vs peers is the first diagnostic; aggressive follow-up on non-filers and short-filers.
  - **Fake-invoice/ITC fraud enforcement** — analytics on the BOIFSCL GSTN data to flag circular trading; most states have found 2-5% of recoverable leakage here.
  - **Sector-specific drives** — construction materials, inter-state e-commerce, jewellery — sectors known for under-declaration.
  - **E-way bill enforcement** at border checkposts with UP, Jharkhand, WB, Nepal.

### Opportunity 3 — Stamps & Registration: **+₹3,671 Cr gap**
- **Bihar today:** ₹6,348 Cr (14% of SOTR, actually higher share than peers' 9%)
- **Peer average:** ₹9,428 Cr; per-capita ₹962 vs Bihar ₹610
- **Potential at peer per-capita rate:** ₹10,019 Cr (58% increase)
- **9-year CAGR:** Bihar 9.97% vs national 11.23% — modestly lagging
- **Why this is the most tractable line:** it is purely a state-administered tax on a transaction that already happens (property transfers); the question is only what rate applies and whether the declared consideration reflects true market value.
- **Policy levers:**
  - **Circle-rate (minimum valuation) revision** — in many Bihar districts circle rates have not been updated since 2014-15, so declared transaction values fall well below market. A data-driven revision using recent registered sale statistics could lift collections 15-25% without raising duty rates.
  - **Plug undervaluation** — random audit of registered deeds against comparable sales; penalty regime for suppressed consideration.
  - **Digitisation + integration** — complete rollout of online registration, link with land records (Bhumi) and property-tax rolls so that unregistered/benami transfers surface.
  - **Extend tax net to indirect transfers** — joint-development agreements, long-term leases, corporate share transfers with real-estate substance.
  - **Special stamp on high-value commercial leases** — currently under-declared.

### Runners-up (mentioned for completeness, not in top 3)
- **Sales/Trade Tax (+₹14,695 Cr):** largely petroleum VAT post-GST. Room exists but is constrained by fuel-price politics and because Bihar already taxes petroleum at above-average rates.
- **Motor Vehicle Tax (+₹3,080 Cr):** smaller gap and Bihar is already growing faster than peers; no urgent reform.
- **State Excise:** see §4.

## 4. Risks and caveats

### Policy-locked revenue
- **Bihar's prohibition policy (2016 onwards)** eliminates ~₹18,000 Cr/year of State Excise that peer states collect. The peer-benchmark analysis mechanically flags this as Bihar's single largest "gap" (₹18,170 Cr). It is not. The revenue is foregone by deliberate policy choice with stated social-welfare objectives. The question for the government is whether those social benefits justify the revenue foregone — not whether to reverse the policy. This analysis excludes State Excise from the tractable-opportunity list on those grounds.
- **Sales/Trade Tax on alcohol** is similarly zero in Bihar, which partly explains the ₹14,695 Cr Sales/Trade gap — not all of it is recoverable even in principle.

### Methodological caveats
- **Per-capita uses Census 2011** (the latest authoritative denominator). Bihar's population has grown faster than peers' over 2011-23, so the true 2023 per-capita gap is somewhat narrower than reported here. A rough adjustment using NCAER projections would shrink the per-capita gaps by 8-12%.
- **"Peer benchmark" is not a ceiling.** Peers are themselves below top performers. Matching the peer group gets Bihar to median, not best-in-class. Maharashtra, Karnataka, Gujarat per-capita SOTR is ~₹22,000-27,000 vs Bihar's ₹4,646.
- **Tax base ≠ population.** Bihar's per-capita GSDP is ~₹54,000 (2022-23) vs peer median ~₹1,20,000. A portion of the "gap" reflects a smaller consumption and asset base, not weaker tax administration. Normalising by GSDP (tax/GSDP ratio) would give a fairer read of tax effort — we do not have GSDP in this workbook (see §5).
- **Central transfers are partly a right, not charity.** Bihar's high share in Union Taxes reflects the Finance Commission formula, which is a constitutional devolution mechanism, not discretionary aid. Reducing transfer dependence is not the same as reducing transfers — it means growing own revenue faster so the *share* of own revenue rises.
- **Non-Tax Revenue gap assumes Bihar has a similar economic base for royalties/fees as peers.** In practice Bihar has less mineral wealth than Jharkhand/Odisha/Chhattisgarh. A realistic capture is probably 30-50% of the headline gap.

### Data-quality caveats carried over from earlier steps
- `State Excise` for Bihar shows ₹1.15 Cr in 2023-24 — technically non-zero but essentially noise from residual pre-prohibition receipts.
- The source workbook has numerous spelling inconsistencies ("Chattisgarh", "Uttrakhand", "Grant in aid-CSS" vs "Grants in Aid - CSS") and duplicate/reconciliation sub-tables that required careful parsing. Values in `data/master_revenue.csv` were spot-checked but not audited line-by-line against the MoF-published PDFs.

## 5. What to investigate next (data we'd want but don't have)

### Tax effort (normalise by economic base)
- **State GSDP** and **per-capita GSDP** for FY 2013-14 through 2023-24. Enables computing tax/GSDP ratio, which is the economist's standard measure of tax effort. Available from MoSPI / RBI Handbook of State Statistics.
- **Sector composition of GSDP** (agri/industry/services split) — explains part of the tax-base gap.

### Drill-down on Non-Tax Revenue (Opportunity 1)
- Sub-component split: interest receipts, dividends, general services (fees), economic services (mining royalties, forestry, industries), social services (education fees, medical). The workbook's `A09_PD_Account`, `A12_GIA`, and `Object Head_Dtld` sheets likely carry this — we have not extracted them.
- Mining royalty rates and production volumes for Bihar's declared minerals.

### GST compliance (Opportunity 2)
- **Number of registered GSTINs per ₹ Cr of GSDP** — proxies for formalisation.
- **GSTR-3B filing rates, short-filer rates, non-filer population.**
- **ITC-to-output-tax ratio** by sector — flags likely leakage.
- **E-way bill volumes** and border-checkpost seizures.

### Stamps & Registration (Opportunity 3)
- **Current circle rates** by district and their age (year last revised).
- **Registered-deed volumes vs market transaction estimates** — a measure of the informal property market.
- Duty rates on different instrument types (sale vs gift vs lease vs mortgage) — Bihar vs peers.

### Expenditure side (not in scope today, but essential for a full fiscal picture)
- **Salary + pension + interest = "committed" expenditure** as a % of revenue receipts. Bihar has high committed expenditure ratios historically; each additional rupee of own revenue matters more than in fiscally roomier states. `A11_Committed Exp` sheet in the workbook.
- **Capex share of total expenditure** — measures how much revenue growth actually flows into development.
- **Fiscal deficit and debt-to-GSDP trajectory** — `A19_FRBM_FA` and `fiscal deficit cal` sheets.

### External benchmarks
- **Tax/GSDP for Bihar vs the FRBM target** for the state.
- **Comparisons from 15th Finance Commission** (2021-26) reports and Bihar's FRBM review documents.
- **RBI's State Finances: A Study of Budgets** — annual publication with state-level fiscal-effort comparisons.
